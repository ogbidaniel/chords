# Circle of Fifths Progressions

Most of the chord movement in popular music — and almost all of it in jazz — moves around the **circle of fifths**. Each chord resolves to one a fifth below it: G7 wants to land on C, D7 wants to land on G, A7 wants to land on D, and so on. Internalize this one pattern and the harmony of thousands of standards becomes legible.

## The single most common move

Two chords moving down a fifth. The dominant 7th resolving to a major chord is the strongest cadence in Western music:

[[G7 → Cmaj7]]

Now hear the same motion from a different root:

[[D7 → Gmaj7]]

And again:

[[C7 → Fmaj7]]

These all sound the same — same shape, different keys. That's the whole game.

## Adding the ii: ii–V–I

Insert the ii chord (minor 7) before the V and you have **the** progression of jazz harmony:

[[Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7]]

The voice leading is what makes it sing. The 7th of [[Dm7]] (the note C) drops a half-step to become the 3rd of [[G7]] (the note B). The 3rd of [[G7]] (also B) drops a half-step to become the 7th of [[Cmaj7]]. Two voices barely move; the chord underneath them changes entirely.

Practice this in another key:

[[Gm7 → C7 → Fmaj7]]

And another:

[[Cm7 → F7 → Bbmaj7]]

If you can play ii–V–I in all twelve keys, you have the foundation of jazz piano.

## The full cycle: ii–V–I, repeating

Now stack ii–V–Is in a cycle. Each new key starts a whole step below the last:

[[Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7 → Cm7 → F7 → Bbmaj7]]

That's the cycle drill in two keys. Keep going and you walk through all twelve. The **Drill** section of this app has a full cycle exercise with progress tracking; do the prose first, then go practice it.

## Half-step motion as substitution

The cycle isn't a prison. One of the most useful jazz substitutions is the **tritone sub**: replace the V chord with the dominant 7th a tritone away. Db7 has the same tritone (F–B) as G7, so it resolves to C just as strongly — but it moves down by half-step instead of fifth:

[[Dm7 → Db7 → Cmaj7]]

Hear the difference. The cycle move is propulsive and resolved. The tritone sub is sleek and chromatic. Both work; jazz pianists use them interchangeably.

## Minor ii–V–i

In a minor key, the formula shifts: the ii becomes half-diminished (m7♭5, written ø) and the V often gets altered tensions:

[[Dø → G7 → Cm7]]

Same root motion, darker color. This is what gives minor-key standards like *Autumn Leaves* and *Blue Bossa* their distinctive sound.

## What to practice

Slowly, with both hands:

1. ii–V–I in C, F, B♭, E♭, A♭, D♭ (flat keys) — the left side of the cycle
2. Then G, D, A, E, B, F♯ (sharp keys) — the right side
3. Then walk the full cycle in order, one ii–V–I per bar
4. When that's smooth, drop the I and just chain ii–V → ii–V → ii–V around the cycle — this is the "rhythm changes bridge" sound

Each ii–V–I lasts about four bars at performance tempo. Building this slowly into muscle memory — different keys, different voicings, different tempos — is the work of the first year of serious jazz piano study.
