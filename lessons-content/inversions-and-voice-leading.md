# Chord Inversions and Smooth Voice Leading

A chord can be played with any of its notes in the bass. **Root position** means the root is the lowest note. **First inversion** puts the 3rd on the bottom. **Second inversion** puts the 5th on the bottom. For 7th chords, **third inversion** puts the 7th on the bottom.

Inversions don't change *what* chord you're playing — they change how it *sits*. And the choice between them is the difference between music that lurches and music that flows.

## The same chord, four ways

Here is [[C]] in root position. Now in first inversion: [[C/E]]. Same three notes, the E on the bottom instead of the C.

For four-note chords like [[Cmaj7]], you have three inversions to choose from. The lowest note shifts the chord's color and weight without changing its identity.

## Why inversions matter — the bass line

Try playing this progression with every chord in root position:

[[C → Am → F → G]]

Now play the same progression with inversions chosen to keep the bass line moving smoothly:

[[C → Am/C → F/C → G/B]]

The first version *lurches* — the bass jumps around in fourths and fifths. The second version glides — C, C, C, B — a single descending step at the end. Same harmony, different motion.

## Voice leading in jazz

In a ii–V–I, voice leading by half-step is the goal. Here is the prescription Dyas teaches in the Monk Institute handout:

> The 7th of one chord moves down a half-step to become the 3rd of the next chord. The 3rd stays put to become the 7th.

Try the progression [[Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7]] and watch where the notes go:

- The 7th of [[Dm7]] is **C**. The 3rd of [[G7]] is **B**. C → B is a half-step down.
- The 3rd of [[Dm7]] is **F**. The 7th of [[G7]] is also **F**. It doesn't move.
- The 7th of [[G7]] is **F**. The 3rd of [[Cmaj7]] is **E**. F → E is a half-step down.
- The 3rd of [[G7]] is **B**. The 7th of [[Cmaj7]] is also **B**. It doesn't move.

Two voices barely budge while the chord changes underneath them. That motionlessness is exactly what gives a well-voiced ii–V–I its sleek, inevitable sound.

## Practice this

1. Play the C-major scale chord progression — [[C]], [[Dm]], [[Em]], [[F]], [[G]], [[Am]], [[Bdim]] — in root position. Hear how angular it sounds.
2. Now play the same progression but keep your right hand on or near middle C the whole time. You'll be forced to use inversions. Hear how it suddenly flows.
3. Try ii–V–I in C: [[Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7]]. Then try it in F: [[Gm7 → C7 → Fmaj7]]. Then in Bb: [[Cm7 → F7 → Bbmaj7]]. Each one applies the same half-step voice-leading rule.
